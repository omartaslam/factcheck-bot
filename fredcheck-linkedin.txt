── LONG-FORM LINKEDIN ARTICLE ──────────────────────

TITLE: Why Western Fact-Checkers Are Failing Global Journalism

A claim spreads across WhatsApp in Lagos. Within hours it's viral across
West Africa. By the time a mainstream fact-checker looks at it — if they
look at it at all — the damage is done.

But here's the deeper problem: even when Western fact-checkers do engage,
they often reach for the same set of sources. Reuters. AP. BBC. The New
York Times. These are credible outlets. But they are not the whole picture.

A story that goes unreported in London or New York isn't necessarily false.
It may simply be inconvenient, niche, or outside the editorial priorities
of a handful of Western gatekeepers.

This is the blind spot at the heart of modern fact-checking.

WHAT GOOD VERIFICATION ACTUALLY REQUIRES

Real verification means casting the net wide. It means checking what Al
Jazeera reported alongside Reuters. What Agencia EFE found alongside AFP.
What regional African outlets documented alongside the BBC.

It means not treating the absence of Western coverage as evidence of
falsehood.

It means not importing political background — "resigned amid controversy,"
"disputed by officials" — to weaken a specific claim that may be entirely
accurate.

And it means being honest about the difference between "we couldn't verify
this" and "this is false."

HOW FRED APPROACHES IT

Fred Check was built around a single principle: no source gets automatic
authority. Not governments. Not wire services. Not official investigations.

Every claim is cross-referenced across 65+ sources spanning Western, Arabic,
Spanish, French and Global South media. Verdicts require corroboration from
independent outlets — not just volume of coverage.

The result is a verdict that reflects the actual state of global evidence,
not the editorial consensus of a few dominant voices.

WHO IT'S FOR

Fred is built for journalists who need fast, sourced verification without
the bias baked into most tools. For press rooms that cover regions where
Western sources are thin. For researchers and activists who need to know
whether a claim holds up — not just whether it's been covered.

It's available on WhatsApp and at fredcheck.com. Free to try.

The question isn't whether bias exists in fact-checking. It's whether you
want a tool that actively works against it.
